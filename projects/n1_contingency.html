Background

What is N-1 contingency analysis?

The N-1 criterion is one of the most fundamental reliability standards in power systems engineering. It states that if any single element in a power system, a transmission line, transformer, or generator, fails unexpectedly, the remaining system must continue operating within safe limits without cascading failures or customer outages.

This standard is mandated in North America by NERC (the North American Electric Reliability Corporation) under Reliability Standard TPL-001-5, which requires transmission planners to demonstrate N-1 security before approving new generation interconnections. Grid operators including PJM, MISO, CAISO, and ERCOT run this analysis as a prerequisite for connecting any new power source, including wind and solar farms, to the transmission grid.

Put simply: before a wind farm gets connected to the grid, someone has to show that if a nearby line trips, nothing else will fail. This project automates that check on a standard benchmark network.

Professional context: At Siemens (2025), this type of contingency analysis was performed in PSS®E, the industry-standard commercial software used by utilities and ISOs globally. Python automation tools built there reduced the runtime of that workflow by approximately 30%. This project replicates the same analytical methodology using pandapower, the open-source equivalent, on a publicly available benchmark network. The engineering logic is identical.
Network data

The IEEE 39-Bus New England Power System

The IEEE 39-bus network, commonly known as the 10-machine New England Power System, is the standard benchmark test case used in this study. It represents a simplified, highly interconnected high-voltage transmission topology originally derived from the actual transmission grid in the New England area of the United States. [1][3]

Key components

  • Generators: 10 synchronous machines. Generator 1 typically represents an aggregation of a large number of generators outside the immediate study area, representing the rest of the interconnection. [1][2]
  • Bus types: 39 buses comprising 10 generator buses, 12 load buses, and 17 constant power (PQ) nodes. [9]
  • Topology: 34 transmission lines and 12 tap-changing transformers forming a highly meshed network typical of high-voltage transmission systems. [6]
  • Geography: Derived from a simplified model of the actual transmission grid in the New England area of the United States. [3][5]

Why this network is used

Researchers and engineers use this network across a wide range of studies. It shows up in: [4][7][8]

  • Transient Stability: Extensively used to study rotor angle dynamics and fault tolerance, for example, simulating a 3-phase to ground fault and observing whether generators remain in synchronism.
  • Optimal Power Flow (OPF): Used to minimize generation costs and test economic dispatch limits across the network.
  • N-1 Contingency Analysis: Standard benchmark for transmission planning studies, which is how it is used in this project.
  • AI and ML research: A popular baseline for training machine learning algorithms to detect grid congestion, voltage instability, and blackout risk, directly relevant to Project 3 (ML-based fault detection) in this portfolio. [7][8]

The network is built directly into pandapower and loads in a single line of code: net = pandapower.networks.case39(), no data download required.

Methodology

What the code does, step by step

Step 1, Base case power flow

The first step loads the IEEE 39-bus network and solves the AC power flow equations to establish a baseline. Power flow finds the voltage magnitude and angle at every bus, and the real and reactive power flowing through every line, given the generation dispatch and load at each bus. The mathematical method used is Newton-Raphson iteration, the same algorithm used by PSS®E in professional grid studies. It starts from a flat start (all voltages at 1.0 pu, all angles at 0°) and iterates until the power mismatch at every bus is below a convergence threshold of 1×10⁻⁸ MVA.

Step 2, N-1 contingency loop

For each of the 35 lines in the network, the code takes that line out, re-solves the power flow, and checks two things. Then it puts the line back and moves to the next one.

  • Thermal criterion (loading > 100%): No remaining line should carry more than 100% of its rated current. Exceeding the thermal rating causes the conductor to sag due to heat expansion, reducing ground clearance. Protection relays will trip the line automatically, potentially starting a cascade.
  • Voltage criterion (outside 0.95–1.05 pu): Every bus must stay within the standard North American operating band. Below 0.95 pu causes equipment stress, motor stall, and eventual voltage collapse. Above 1.05 pu risks insulation breakdown on transformers and cables.

Step 3, Wind penetration scenario

Three generators (at buses 30, 31, and 32) are treated as variable wind turbines. Their output is varied from 20% to 100% of rated capacity in five steps. At each wind level, the entire N-1 contingency sweep is repeated, 35 outages re-tested, to produce a full picture of how increasing wind generation changes the number and severity of reliability violations across the network.

Results

What the results show

N-1 base case, severity ranking

Line 26 is the weakest point in this network. When it trips, another line jumps to 157.5% of its thermal rating. In a real grid that second line would trip within seconds on its protection relay. You are now looking at a potential cascade. This is exactly the type of finding that triggers a reinforcement study: where do we need to build new lines or upgrade existing ones?

As wind generation increases from 20% to 80% of rated output, N-1 violations drop from 35 to 8, a 77% reduction. This happens because higher wind output from distributed generators reduces the power flowing through the most heavily loaded transmission corridors, relieving thermal pressure on critical lines.

However, at 100% wind output, violations tick back up slightly to 10. This reflects a real phenomenon in high-renewable grids: as power flow patterns shift with very high wind penetration, new stress points emerge on lines that were previously lightly loaded. Transmission planners call this a topology change in the power flow pattern, and it is one of the central engineering challenges in integrating large amounts of variable renewable generation into existing transmission infrastructure.
